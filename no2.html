Nitrogen dioxide NO₂

General informations and impacts

Nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) is a major air pollutant and one of the most harmful components of nitrogen oxides (NOₓ), produced mainly by the combustion of fossil fuels. It is emitted by cars, trucks, buses, power plants and various industrial sources. In the atmosphere, NO₂ plays a central role in air pollution through several harmful processes. It contributes significantly to the formation of ground-level ozone and fine particulate matter, both of which can penetrate deep into the lungs and aggravate respiratory diseases such as asthma.

Environmentally, NO₂ reacts with other atmospheric compounds to create acid rain, which damages forests, soils, and aquatic ecosystems. It also leads to the formation of nitrate particles that contribute to atmospheric haze, reducing visibility—especially in natural areas like national parks. NO₂ is also a key precursor of photochemical smog, a reddish-brown haze observed in many cities, especially under sunny and stagnant weather conditions. This smog results from complex reactions involving NOₓ, hydrocarbons, and sunlight, producing ozone and other irritants harmful to both humans and the environment.

Situation in Kosovo

In Kosovo, nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) is recognized as a key air pollutant, monitored alongside particulate matter, sulfur dioxide, ozone, and carbon monoxide at a network of 12 stations managed by the Kosovo Hydrometeorological Institute and the Kosovo Environmental Protection Agency. NO₂ concentrations are measured hourly and contribute directly to the national Air Quality Index. The main emission sources include road traffic, coal-fired power plants (Kosovo A and Kosovo B), residential heating, and industrial activities.

In urban areas, especially in Pristina, NO₂ levels are particularly high due to heavy traffic and the use of low-quality fuels: about 52% of NO₂ pollution originates from transport, 28% from energy production, and 11% from industry. Monitoring data (e.g., in the ex Rilindja area) have recorded hourly peaks in NO₂ levels of 100–132 µg/m³, significantly above WHO and EU guidelines, especially during winter. Although annual averages (around 20–21 µg/m³) sometimes stay below material protection limits (40 µg/m³), short term winter spikes often exceed health thresholds by several times. These elevated levels drive wintertime smog, acidifying aerosols (including nitrate particles), and heightened respiratory risks, particularly in urban zones with dense traffic and coal combustion.
